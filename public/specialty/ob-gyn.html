Compensation by Experience Level

Early career OB-GYN Physicians (1-3 years post-training) typically earn 15-20% below the specialty average as they build patient volume and referral networks. Mid-career practitioners (8-15 years) earn at or above average, with peak earnings occurring between years 12-20. Late-career physicians may see gradual declines as they reduce operative volume or call coverage. The 25th-to-75th percentile range spans roughly $150,000-$200,000 within OB/GYN, reflecting enormous variation based on geography, practice model, and patient mix.

Top-Paying States

After adjusting for cost of living and state income tax, the highest-paying states for OB-GYN Physicians are Texas (no state tax, moderate COL, strong tort reform), Tennessee (no state tax, low COL), Indiana (3% tax, very low COL), and Oklahoma (low COL despite moderate tax). Academic centers in Boston, New York, and San Francisco typically pay 20-35% below private practice benchmarks. Rural and semi-rural markets offer 15-25% premiums over urban areas, with signing bonuses of $25,000-$75,000 and loan repayment incentives.

Employed vs Private Practice

Hospital-employed OB-GYN Physicians earn a predictable salary with benefits and malpractice coverage, but typically 15-30% less than private practice colleagues. Private practice OB/GYN physicians bear business risk and overhead (typically 45-65% of revenue) but benefit from higher income ceilings, equity value in their practice, and potential ASC or ancillary service revenue. The gap is most pronounced in procedural specialties where ancillary revenue streams can add $100,000-$400,000 annually on top of professional fees.
